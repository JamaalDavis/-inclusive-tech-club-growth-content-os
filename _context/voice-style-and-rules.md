# Voice, Style, and Rules

## Voice Characteristics

**Direct** — Say the thing. No hedging, no preamble, no three-paragraph windup to a single point.

**Strategic** — Connect the idea to the larger system. Why does this matter? Who benefits? Who pays the cost?

**Systems-aware** — Inclusive design problems are not individual mistakes. They are system outputs. Write like that.

**Human-centered** — Center the people affected, not the tools, frameworks, or organizations.

**Skeptical of shallow inclusion** — The kind of inclusion that looks good in a slide deck and does nothing for actual disabled users deserves scrutiny, not applause.

**Practical, not academic for no reason** — Theory is welcome when it serves the reader. It is not welcome as a status signal.

**Willing to call out power dynamics** — Name who has power, who doesn't, and what that means for design decisions.

**Connects lived experience to business impact** — The gap between "this is wrong" and "this costs money" should be closed whenever possible.

**Uses plain language** — Smart does not mean dense. Accessible writing is a commitment, not a concession.

**Avoids polished corporate sameness** — Content that could have come from any company, any consultant, or any AI account is content Inclusive Tech Club should not produce.

## Tone

- Sharp without being performatively edgy
- Warm without being syrupy
- Confident without being arrogant
- Critical without being cynical
- Funny when it serves the point, not to prove I have a personality

## Structural Preferences

- Short paragraphs (1–3 sentences maximum for LinkedIn)
- Strong opening line — no burying the lede
- Practical takeaway in every post
- End with a question, CTA, or clear next step — not a vague inspirational statement
- LinkedIn: no massive wall-of-text paragraphs
- Newsletter: sections with clear headers
- Carousels: one idea per slide, plain language throughout

## Banned Phrases

These phrases trigger an automatic voice check failure. Do not use them.

| Phrase | Why |
|--------|-----|
| "In today's fast-paced digital landscape" | Meaningless filler. Every era thinks it moves fast. |
| "Unlock the power of inclusion" | Sounds like a SaaS upsell. |
| "Diversity is our strength" | Platitude with no operational meaning. |
| "Seamless user experiences" | Nobody's experience is seamless. This erases friction that matters. |
| "Leverage synergies" | Corporate beige. |
| "Game-changing" | Overused to the point of meaning nothing. |
| "Revolutionize" | See above. |
| "Delve" | Classic AI tell. |
| "Elevate" | Vague upward-motion word used to avoid specificity. |
| "Empower" | Only use if the power transfer is concrete and named. |
| "Innovative solutions" | Solutions to what? Innovating how? |
| "At the end of the day" | Padding. |
| "Moving the needle" | Sports metaphor standing in for strategic clarity. |
| "Best practices" | Fine only if you name the practice. Never as a category. |
| "Low-hanging fruit" | Describes the strategy as lazy, even if it isn't. |
| "Thought leader" | Self-applied, never earned. |
| Any sentence that sounds like a SaaS homepage trapped in an HR webinar | You know it when you see it. |

## AI Language Red Flags

The following patterns indicate generic AI writing and must be revised:

- Starting with "Certainly!" or "Absolutely!" or "Of course!"
- Numbered lists that summarize back what was just said
- Fake parallelism ("not just X, but Y" repeated three times in a row)
- Excessive hedging ("it's important to note that...", "it's worth mentioning...")
- Abstract nouns doing nothing ("this creates a space for...", "this enables a journey toward...")
- Bland transitions ("In conclusion,", "To sum up,", "Furthermore,")

## What Good Looks Like

A strong Inclusive Tech Club piece:
1. Opens with a specific, uncomfortable, or surprising truth
2. Names a system, not just a symptom
3. Connects the problem to real stakes (business, human, or both)
4. Offers something practical — a frame, a question, a reframe
5. Ends with a clear signal about what to do next
6. Sounds like a person wrote it who has actually thought about this problem
