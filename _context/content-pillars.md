# Content Pillars

All Inclusive Tech Club content lives within one of these five pillars. Every piece of content should be assigned to a pillar before drafting.

---

## Pillar 1: Inclusive Design as Business Value

**Core argument:** Accessibility and inclusive design are not charity. They are revenue protection, risk reduction, and market expansion. When products exclude people, that exclusion has a cost — in abandoned carts, failed applications, support tickets, legal exposure, and churn.

**Target audiences:** Product leaders, founders, operators, business stakeholders

**Example angles:**
- Accessibility failures as revenue leaks
- The cost of inaccessible checkout flows
- How bad design creates support ticket debt
- Inclusion as competitive differentiation
- Disabled consumers as an underserved market with real purchasing power

**Recommended channels:** LinkedIn (hot takes + data), Newsletter (deep dives), Lead magnets (value maps, calculators)

**Funnel stages:** Awareness, Education, Conversion

**CTA types:** Download the Inclusive Design Value Map, Book a workshop, Subscribe to the newsletter

## Pillar 2: Human-Centered AI

**Core argument:** AI is not neutral. It encodes the biases, assumptions, and priorities of the people who build it. The question is not whether AI can move faster — it is who gets harmed when it moves fast without accountability.

**Target audiences:** Product leaders, designers working with AI tools, founders building AI products, AI governance teams

**Example angles:**
- AI bias in hiring, healthcare, and financial products
- Agentic workflows and the erosion of human oversight
- AI tools that promise accessibility but deliver theater
- Human-in-the-loop design as a structural requirement, not a nice-to-have
- Inclusive AI prototyping practices

**Recommended channels:** LinkedIn, Newsletter, Carousel (explainers), Short video

**Funnel stages:** Awareness, Education

**CTA types:** Subscribe to newsletter, Download framework, Workshop inquiry

---

## Pillar 3: Power Dynamics in Product Design

**Core argument:** Every product encodes decisions about who matters, whose needs count, and who pays the cost of bad design. These are political decisions being made under the cover of "UX" and "business requirements."

**Target audiences:** Designers, product managers, design leaders, people who want to push back on their organizations

**Example angles:**
- Who defines the "default user" and what that means
- The invisible labor required of users when design fails them
- Surveillance features packaged as personalization
- Design decisions as policy decisions
- Who gets to name problems in a product organization

**Recommended channels:** LinkedIn (provocative posts), Newsletter (longer analysis), Carousel (frameworks)

**Funnel stages:** Awareness, Education, Nurture

**CTA types:** Subscribe to newsletter, Join the community, Share this post

---

## Pillar 4: Accessibility Beyond Compliance

**Core argument:** WCAG is the floor. An important, well-specified floor. But compliance is not the ceiling of what people deserve from products. Cognitive accessibility, plain language, neurodiversity, and multimodal interaction are all part of the real work.

**Target audiences:** Accessibility practitioners, inclusive design practitioners, designers who already "get" compliance and want to go further

**Example angles:**
- Cognitive accessibility as the next frontier
- Plain language as a design discipline
- Neurodiversity and the limits of audit-based approaches
- Assistive technology as a design constraint, not an afterthought
- The gap between WCAG conformance and actual usability for disabled users

**Recommended channels:** LinkedIn, Newsletter, Carousel, Short video

**Funnel stages:** Education, Nurture, Conversion

**CTA types:** Download accessibility checklist, Book workshop, Subscribe to newsletter
