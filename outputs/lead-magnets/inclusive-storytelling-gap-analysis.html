Inclusive Storytelling Accessibility Gap Analysis

Where legacy accessibility approaches fall short — and what to do next

Gap analysis helps move teams from compliance add-ons to inclusive system design.

The Five Gaps

Five areas where legacy accessibility approaches break in inclusive storytelling, with the impact of each gap and the recommended shift.
Area ✗Current gap !Impact →Recommended shift Priority
1 Entry points Gap Access options are hidden or unavailable at the start Impact Users face friction before they can engage Shift Present modality choices early and clearly High
2 Content delivery Gap Story is designed for one dominant mode, usually text or visuals Impact Comprehension drops for users with different needs or contexts Shift Plan text, audio, captions, and assistive pathways together High
3 Transitions across modes Gap Switching formats causes loss of place, progress, or meaning Impact Users must restart or reconstruct context Shift Preserve continuity across modalities High
4 Testing and validation Gap Teams rely on checklists or automated scans only Impact Lived experience gaps remain invisible Shift Test with disabled, neurodivergent, multilingual, and edge-case users High
5 Ownership and governance Gap Accessibility is siloed or added late Impact Inclusion becomes expensive cleanup and uneven quality Shift Assign shared ownership across design, content, engineering, research, and leadership Medium–High

Signals of Maturity

Teams moving from compliance add-ons to inclusive system design show these patterns:

  • ↑ Accessibility decisions happen upstream
  • ◎ Teams measure comprehension, not just compliance
  • ⇄ Users can change modes without losing meaning
  • ◈ Inclusive design is shared across functions

it is trustworthy meaning across forms."
